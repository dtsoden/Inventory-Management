# Shane Young's Power Platform Demo vs. Pro-Code with AI

## The 60-Second Summary

Shane Young built a complete inventory management solution in Microsoft Power Platform across model-driven apps, canvas apps, Power Automate, AI Builder, Copilot Studio, and a mobile app. We replicated every feature in pro-code (Next.js 15, TypeScript, SQLite, OpenAI) in **roughly 4.5 to 5 hours of AI-assisted development with human-in-the-loop testing and validation**, and we shipped MORE than Shane did, including a full Docusaurus documentation site that ships with the app.

This page is a direct, feature-by-feature accounting. It is also a business argument. Shane's craft is not in question. The platform underneath it is.

## What We Shipped That Shane Did Not

Parity was the floor. The replica exceeds Shane's demo on the following dimensions, all built inside the same 4.5 to 5 hour window.

- **Full Docusaurus documentation site** with separate User Guide and Admin Guide, role-gated, accessible from a help icon in the header. Shane has no formal documentation; the training is sold as a live class. Our app ships its own manual.
- **Single-tenant white-label branding** with custom logo, light and dark primary colors, favicon, and theme mode lock. The product is drop-in brandable for any reseller or internal org.
- **Encrypted secrets vault** with passphrase-derived AES-256-GCM. API keys and sensitive integration credentials never sit in plaintext in the database.
- **Last-admin protection.** The system refuses to deactivate, delete, or demote the final remaining admin. No self-inflicted lockouts.
- **Real Code 128 plus QR barcodes** generated via JsBarcode with screen-scannable bar widths. Shane's were placeholders printed on a sheet. Ours scan reliably from both phone cameras and Netum C750 USB scanners.
- **Idempotent runtime database migrations** on container startup, so user data is never wiped during deploys. This is a hard rule: production data persists across every upgrade.
- **Profile avatar upload with live header refresh.** Users upload a picture, the header reflects it immediately, no reload needed.
- **Full Manufacturer entity** for complete provenance chain (Manufacturer to Vendor to Item to PurchaseOrderLine to Asset). Shane skipped manufacturer, which is fine for a demo and a problem in production.
- **Setup wizard for fresh installs.** First-run experience walks a new operator through org creation, admin account, integrations, and CORS in minutes.
- **Multi-tenancy, RBAC, audit logging, notifications, dashboard KPIs, CSV export, item categories with hierarchy.** Enterprise basics that were absent from the demo because the demo was a teaching vehicle, not a product.

The pro-code build is a one-time engagement plus an OpenAI bill that scales with actual usage. For most inventory workloads, the OpenAI line item is a rounding error.

## The Strategic Reality

This section is not about Shane. It is about the ground shifting underneath every low-code practice in the industry. Shane happens to be one of the most visible figures in that space, which is exactly why his demo is the right target.

Pro code with AI is now faster to develop than low code. Full stop. The only reason low code ever existed was to compress development time for line-of-business applications. Drag-and-drop, wizards, and generative pages were the answer to "writing code takes too long." That answer was correct for about fifteen years. It is not correct anymore. AI compressed pro-code development by an order of magnitude, and in doing so it removed the single disadvantage that pro code had versus low code. Pro code kept every advantage it ever had (portability, source control, unlimited customization, no license tax, no vendor lock-in) and picked up the one thing it was missing (speed). The competitive picture inverted quietly over the last eighteen months, and most of the industry has not caught up yet.

Gartner's own positioning has already started shifting low-code and no-code platforms from "Strategic" toward "On borrowed time." Power Platform, OutSystems, Mendix, Appian, Retool at the heavier tier, all of them were rated as the future of enterprise application delivery as recently as 2023. The current analyst narrative is materially different. The phrase that keeps appearing is "plateau," followed by "decline." Companies whose entire delivery thesis is built on one of these platforms are looking at a plateau by end of year and a measurable decline shortly after. This is not a prediction; it is already visible in new-logo acquisition rates and in the hiring patterns of the companies that used to buy these platforms the loudest.

Here is the financial trap that almost nobody in the low-code consulting business wants to say out loud. Every Power Platform deliverable is a permanent annuity flowing OUT of the customer's bank account and INTO Microsoft's. Per-user licensing, premium connectors, AI Builder credits, Dataverse capacity, Copilot add-ons: every one of those line items is recurring revenue that the consultant helped set up and does not receive a cent of. A Power Platform consultant is, economically, a very well-paid sales engineer for Microsoft licensing. The customer pays the consultant once and pays Microsoft forever. That "forever" revenue could, instead, be flowing to the consultant.

The opportunity is straightforward. Instead of teaching customers to license Power Platform, Shane (or any low-code practice owner) can deliver the same solutions as pro-code SaaS that he hosts and supports. Same outcome for the customer, same domain expertise on the delivery side, same trusted-advisor relationship. The recurring revenue lands in the consultant's bank account instead of Microsoft's. The consultant owns the customer relationship, owns the data, owns the upgrade path, owns the SLA. The customer gets a lower total cost of ownership and no vendor lock-in, which means they renew because they want to, not because switching is prohibitively expensive. Both sides win. Microsoft is the only loser, which is survivable.

Support and maintenance, in this model, is a moat rather than a tax. On a Power Platform engagement, support work is constrained by what Microsoft allows and priced against what Microsoft charges. On a pro-code build in your own name, support work is the business. You control the roadmap, the SLA, the release cadence, the integration calendar, and the upsell path. Every customer success conversation is with you, not with a Microsoft account rep who is trying to shift them up a license tier. The consultant becomes the software vendor, which is where the valuation multiples actually live.

The hardest part of this transition is psychological, not technical. A decade of muscle memory says "AI cannot build production apps," "pro code is slow," "low code is the fastest path to delivery," and "Microsoft is the safe choice." Every one of those statements was true in 2022. None of them is true in 2026. The practitioners who update their priors first win the next decade. The ones who wait get priced out by the ones who did not wait.

The replica you are reading about right now exists to make this concrete. Five hours of build time. One developer with AI. Every feature Shane demonstrated, plus a documentation site, plus branding, plus encryption, plus real barcodes, plus migration safety, plus manufacturer provenance. No Power Apps premium license, no AI Builder credits, no Dataverse capacity, no tenant lock-in. If you are a low-code practice owner reading this, the question is not whether the shift is happening. The question is which side of it you want to be on when your customers start asking why their monthly Microsoft bill keeps going up.

### Licensing Tiers

To make this flexible for different customer sizes and procurement realities, the offer is structured as two distinct licensing tiers. Both tiers leave Shane free to wrap hosting, support, and maintenance around the deal as a separate revenue stream.

**Tier 1: Internal-Use License (default)**

We retain full intellectual property rights to the source code by default. The customer receives a perpetual internal-use license, meaning they can run the application for their own business purposes without restriction, but they do not receive the source code and they cannot resell, sublicense, or distribute it. This is the lower price point and the most common path. Shane can layer on a hosting plan and a maintenance agreement on top of this license, and that ongoing services revenue is also split **60/40 in our favor (Shane keeps 40%)**. This tier is ideal for the mid-market customer who just wants the working solution and a phone number to call when something breaks.

**Tier 2: Source Code License (premium)**

For larger enterprises, regulated industries, or customers whose procurement teams insist on owning the code they run, we offer a non-exclusive source code license at a significantly higher price point. The customer receives the full source code and the legal right to modify it, fork it, run it in their own tenant, and use it as the basis for their own internal development. The license is **non-exclusive**, meaning we explicitly retain the right to continue using, recycling, and reusing any part of that codebase in any future product, customer engagement, or commercial offering. They own a copy and the freedom to do what they want with it. We keep the freedom to keep building on it. Both sides win. This tier carries a much higher price tag, much higher margin per deal, and is the right answer for any customer who has the budget and the desire to bring the platform fully in-house. Shane can still offer to host and maintain it for them after delivery, and that hosting and maintenance contract is also split **60/40 in our favor**, regardless of which tier the underlying license is.

**Why this matters in practice**

A lot of enterprise customers are going to want to deploy this in their own Azure or AWS tenant for compliance, data residency, or internal IT policy reasons. Tier 2 gives them a clean, lawyer-friendly way to do that without anyone losing control of the upside. A lot of smaller customers just want it to work and don't care about the source code. Tier 1 gives them an affordable on-ramp and gives Shane a steady, low-friction recurring revenue stream. Either way, Shane gets to walk into the customer conversation with options, not a take-it-or-leave-it pitch. Options close deals.
